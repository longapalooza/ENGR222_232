CLOSED SYSTEM ENERGY BALANCES¶

Recall the energy balance equation governed by the 1st Law of Thermodynamics: $$ Q - W = m \Delta u $$ where $ Q $ is the heat transfer into the system, $ W $ is the work done by the system, $ m $ is the mass of the system, and $ \Delta u $ is the change in specific internal energy of the system.

Also, recall that if a process involves any kind of expansion or compression (i.e. one of the system boundaries is moving), we can calculate the boundary work $ W_b $ as: $$ W_b = \int_{1}^{2} PdV $$ where $ P $ is the pressure of the system and $ V $ is the volume of the system.

We will analyze a spring-loaded piston cylinder-like device where water will undergo an expansion process by means of heat addition. A known mass of water was placed into the cylinder, and the temperature and pressure of the system as well as the change in volume (i.e. height change) of the system were measured as heat was added. We can look at how pressure and volume are related throughout the process to determine the boundary work. We can then perform an energy balance on the system to determine the amount of heat added over the course of the process.

The piston-cylinder shown has two unique features above the piston: a spring and an enclosed mass of trapped air. As the cylinder is heated up (assume only the water is heated up), the water will begin to expand (i.e. vaporize) pushing the piston up. But as the water pushes the piston up, there is increasing resistance from the spring (recall Hooke’s Law for springs) and the air (recall Boyle’s Law and the Ideal Gas Equation).

Using the data given to you (under Lab2 on WeBWorK), you will calculate the boundary work done by the water and the heat absorbed by the water.
To perform your energy balance, you will need to find the internal energy of the water at the various points where data was collected. Note: Due to measurement error, the pressure in your data will probably not line up with the saturated pressure at the given temperature.

How to compute select columns in the Processed Data table (watch units!)¶

  • Specific volume. Compute it from the volume and mass.
  • Boundary work. This is the area under the $ P $ - $ V $ curve from the beginning up to the current data point. (The first row is zero since nothing has happened yet.) To compute each subsequent row, we will assume that the curve is straight from one data point to the next: Multiply the average pressure (of this point and the previous point), by the difference in volume (this gives the work done between these two points). Then add the boundary work from the previous row. This calculation is done the same way for the boundary work of water and of air.
  • Work done on spring. Hint: The force exerted by the water is balanced by the forces from the air and the spring.
  • $ \nu_f $, $ \nu_g $, $ u_f $, $ u_g $. These values must be interpolated from the reference book values above and below your temperature. Notice that the range of temperatures we have are not all in between the same two rows in the reference book.
  • Quality. Compute it from $ \nu_f $, $ \nu_g $, and the current value of $ \nu $.
  • Internal energy. Compute it from the quality, $ u_f $, and $ u_g $.
  • Heat added to the water. Use the First Law as stated in this document. For each row, use the prior data point as the initial state and the current row as the final state. (The first row is zero, since no heat was added yet.) Then add the prior row’s heat to get the total heat added.
